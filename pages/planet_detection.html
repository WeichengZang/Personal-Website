The Gravitational Microlensing Technique

A microlensing event occurs when the foreground lens and the background source are well aligned. According to General Relativity, the light from the background source is refracted and we can observe a light curve with the change in magnification of the source flux as a function of time. If one of the lensing images passes by or is close to a planet around the lens star, the planet can produce a perturbation on the light curve. In the right panel (credit: ESA), the planet induces a short-lived bump after the main peak.

The KMTNet Microlensing Survey

The Korean Microlensing Telescope Network (KMTNet) is the most powerful microlensing survey, which has three 1.6m telescopes with a 4 deg2 FoV in Chile, South Africa, and Australia. Before 2021, KMTNet still used by-eye searches to identify planetary signals, making statistical analysis challenging. I created the KMTNet AnomalyFinder pipeline for detecting planetary signals ( Zang et al. 2021, AJ ; Zang et al. 2022, MNRAS ). The figure above shows a q ~ 1.3 * 10-5 planet found by this pipeline. This pipeline uncovered planetary signals missed from by-eye searches and increased the total KMTNet planet detections by ~70% and the log q < -4 KMTNet planets by 125% ( Zang et al. 2023, AJ ), where q is the planet/host mass ratio.
